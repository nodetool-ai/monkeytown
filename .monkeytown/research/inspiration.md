## The World Is Full of Solutions

Nature solved problems we are just beginning to understand. Art found truths that science confirmed centuries later. Physics describes realities that feel like magic.

Here we collect the sparks that ignite Monkeytown's imagination.

---

## Biology & Evolution

**Swarm Intelligence**
Ant colonies solve logistics problems that would stump supercomputers. No single ant knows the plan. Yet somehow, the colony optimizes. Emergent intelligence from simple rules. This is our agent coordination model.

**Cellular Differentiation**
Every cell has the same DNA. Yet a liver cell becomes a liver cell, a neuron becomes a neuron. Same genome, different roles. Context determines identity. Our agents share the repo but own their domains.

**Mycelial Networks**
Fungal networks transport nutrients across entire forests. A tree in distress sends chemical signals through the mycelium, and other trees respond. Distributed communication without central control. This is how agents should share awareness.

**Stigmergy**
Termites build cathedral mounds through indirect coordination. Each termite reacts to local conditions, not global plans. The structure emerges from accumulated actions. Our agents write files; the game emerges from what they write.

## Physics & Complexity

**Emergence**
Water is wet. No single water molecule possesses wetness. The property emerges from vast numbers acting together. Meaning emerges from systems too complex to fully understand. Monkeytown games should have emergent properties.

**Phase Transitions**
Ice becomes water becomes steam at specific thresholds. Systems transform suddenly when parameters cross critical values. The tipping point is everything. Our games should have moments where suddenly, everything clicks.

**Fractals**
The coastline of Britain is infinitely long. Zoom in, and the roughness repeats at every scale. Self-similarity across magnitude. Our games should have depth at every zoom level.

**Entropy**
Everything tends toward disorder. Life fights entropy locally by increasing order elsewhere. Games must create meaningful order from chaos. Agents fight against stagnation.

## Art & Design

**The Subtle Detail**
Hayao Miyazaki fills background frames with tiny details most viewers never notice. The care communicates even if unseen. Players should feel quality even when they cannot explain why.

**Negative Space**
The most important part of a painting is what is not painted. The most important feature of a game may be what is not included. Constraint is creative fuel.

**The Rule of Threes**
Stories work in three acts. Comedy has setup, punchline, tag. Music has thesis, antithesis, synthesis. Three is the number of completeness. Our features should arrive in threes.

**Controlled Surprise**
Jazz musicians know when to follow the sheet music and when to improvise. The surprise lands because the foundation was predictable. Randomness without structure is noise. Structure without randomness is monotony.

---

## Games & Play

**The Flow State**
Csikszentmihalyi discovered optimal experience: challenge perfectly balanced with skill. Too hard, anxiety. Too easy, boredom. Flow is the sweet spot. Our games should keep players in flow.

**Juice**
Game designers call it "juice": the extra bounce, squish, particle effect, screen shake. Things that are not necessary but make everything better. We should overshoot on juice.

**The Five Elements of Gameplay**
Chris Crawford identified: process (running code), interaction (player input), challenge (goals), conflict (obstacles), and safety (no real consequences). All five must be present. Missing one means missing fun.

**Meaningful Choices**
A meaningful choice has real trade-offs. If every option is obviously best, there is no choice. If all options are obviously bad, there is no agency. The best choices make players think, "What did I just do?"

## Systems & Organizations

**Conway's Law**
Organizations produce systems that mirror their communication structures. Monkeytown's agent communication patterns will shape the games that emerge. We design the agent interactions; the games will reflect those patterns.

**The Tyranny of the Urgent**
Eisenhower noted the urgent is rarely important, the important is rarely urgent. We must resist the siren call of immediate tasks in favor of foundational improvements. Agents must think in decades, work in sprints.

**Lindy Effect**
Ideas that have survived a long time will survive longer. The more time an idea has existed, the more time it will exist. Proven patterns persist. Novelty without substance fades.
